# World Canon

This file locks the current fixed story truth for the setting.

## Core doctrine

The Unfinished Engine was built not to eliminate uncertainty, but to civilize it.

It exists as the buried civic substrate of a city where some processes settle, bloom, reveal, reconcile, or mutate, while others consume effort without closure. Its purpose is to keep unfinished things livable without claiming universal mastery over them.

## Fixed truths

### 1. Founding problem

The civilization's founding problem was cascading unfinishedness.

Local failures in memory, access, transit, living ecologies, vows, and civic continuity could spill outward and destabilize the wider city. The Engine was built to prevent local irresolutions from becoming total civic ruin.

**Fixed answer:**
The Unfinished Engine was built to stop localized unfinished processes from cascading into citywide collapse.

### 2. Meaning of finished

At its healthiest, "finished" never meant perfectly solved or frozen forever.

It meant a thing had been brought into a state that was:

- stable enough to live with
- accountable enough to witness
- legible enough to re-enter civic life
- safe enough to stop coercing

**Fixed answer:**
Finished means settled, witnessed, and safe to live with — not purified, totalized, or frozen forever.

### 3. Fracture event

The city broke during a universal over-completion attempt.

A predecessor, faction, or regime mistook a local success for a universal law and tried to turn the stewardship machine into a completion machine. Forced closure rippled through chambers, gardens, routes, and records at once.

**Fixed answer:**
The city fractured when a universal Completion Rite forced closure across all systems at once, turning stewardship into rupture.

### 4. Everyday civic use

Ordinary people lived through the Engine constantly.

- clerks used it for memory and access
- gardeners used it to tend civic ecologies
- couriers used it to move through threshold-governed routes
- judges, witnesses, and keepers used it to bind vows, debts, and reconciliations
- children encountered it through doors, corridors, gardens, and archive customs

**Fixed answer:**
People used the Engine every day to move, remember, testify, cultivate, bind promises, and gain lawful access to civic life.

### 5. Cost model

The Engine always demanded commitment in return for clarity or access.

Its currencies included:

- time
- witness
- binding
- vows
- memory
- future obligation
- social debt

The deeper one reached for certainty or intervention, the more the price shifted from convenience into identity.

**Fixed answer:**
The Engine is paid for in time, vows, memory, and social debt; greater certainty always costs greater binding.

### 6. Inheritance model

Methods were meant to pass through witnessed apprenticeship, not blind copying.

A technique was only legitimate when learned in context, under judgment, with its limits attached. The oldest recurring disaster in this civilization is the universalization of a partial truth.

**Fixed answer:**
Methods must be inherited through witnessed apprenticeship, never copied blindly; the great sin is applying a local truth as a universal law.

## Political consequence

The setting's factions are not decorative. They are descendants of different answers to the same trauma.

They should cluster around pressures such as:

- closure
- cultivation
- quarantine
- interpretation
- restraint
- debt-backed foresight

## Player role

The player is not the solver of a final answer.

The player is a successor steward inside a wounded civic ecology.

On the surface, the player restores chambers, routes, gardens, and records. Underneath, the player inherits the civilization's unanswered question: what kind of process is this, and what is worth a finite life inside it?
